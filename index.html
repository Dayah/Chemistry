Periodic Trends

The radius of an atom increases as you go down the periodic table because more primary energy levels are added, which are increasingly farther away from the nucleus. The radius decreases as you go across the periodic table. This is a result of more electrons and protons on the same energy levels. Because they are not being added to higher energy levels, the radius will not get larger. Also, because there is more mass, there is more strong nuclear force, so the electrons will be pulled in closer.

Ionization energy increases as you go across the periodic table because the electrons in the outer energy level increase as you move across the table. If an element has more electrons in its outer level, it will try to shield them from other atoms trying to get them. The ionization energy decreases as you go down the periodic table because the electron farthest from the nucleus is easily taken away.

Electron Affinity increases as you go across the periodic table because atoms get smaller and so he strong nuclear charge increases. Electron affinities decrease as you go down the periodic table because the atoms get bigger.

The ionic size of an atom decreases as you go across the periodic table until you reach Group 5. Then, the ionic size gets much larger, and slowly decreases as you go across the periodic table. This strange jump in size is because positive ions are formed to the left of Group 5, and negative ions are formed in Groups 5 and beyond. Negative ions are larger because there is less strong nuclear force for more electrons. As you go down the periodic table, ionic size increases.

Electronegativity increases as you go across the periodic table and decreases as you go down the periodic table. Transition and inner transition elements do not follow the periodic trends as well as the representative elements.
